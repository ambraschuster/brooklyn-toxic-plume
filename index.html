Greenpoint’s Hidden Toxic Threat:
What Residents Should Know

Greenpoint residents may live on contaminated ground but landlords aren’t required to tell them. Now, the community
fights for a Right-to-Know Law.

By Ambra Schuster

Published Oct. 28, 2025

The Meeker Avenue Plume, a large underground contamination site left by the neighborhood’s history of dry cleaning and metalworking industry, was first discovered in 2007 and got declared a national Superfund site in 2022. This means it is officially one of the most hazardous toxic waste sites in the U.S. and gets prioritized for cleanup under federal oversight and funding. Since then, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency has been in charge of investigating the full extent of the contamination and will ultimately develop a cleanup plan.

Despite being around for so long and affecting up to 1,000 buildings, many people living and working in and around the Meeker Avenue Plume still don’t know that they are on contaminated ground. They don’t know about the invisible chemical vapors that can seep through foundation cracks and dirt floors into their homes. And they are unaware of the health hazards these vapors can cause.

They don’t know because many landlords in the area have looked the other way, especially because they are not required by law to inform tenants about the potential harms of living near, or directly on top of, a Superfund site. Moreover, their resistance to get their houses tested stalls remediation and the cleanup process.
